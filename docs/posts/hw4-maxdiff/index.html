Introduction

This report measures how strongly MSBA students at UCSD Rady prefer one core class over another. The data come from a MaxDiff survey (also called Best–Worst Scaling). On each screen the respondent sees a small subset of classes — here, four — and is asked to pick the single most-preferred and the single least-preferred class in that set. Repeating this over many screens, and across many respondents, builds up a detailed picture of relative preference.

Why MaxDiff rather than, say, asking people to rate each class 1–10? Humans are reliable at trade-offs — “this one is better than that one” — but unreliable at calibrating an absolute numeric scale. One respondent’s “7” is another’s “9”, some people never use the extremes, and ratings drift over the course of a survey. By forcing a best and a worst pick out of a small set, MaxDiff removes scale-use bias and makes everyone genuinely differentiate between the items.

The ten items here are the ten core MSBA classes. I estimate students’ relative preference for them three ways, and compare what each method tells us:

  1. A simple counts analysis — the % of times a class is picked best minus the % of times it is picked worst.
  2. The multinomial logit (MNL) model fit by maximum likelihood (MLE), which models the best/worst choices explicitly and gives classical standard errors.
  3. The same MNL model fit by Bayesian methods, via a random-walk Metropolis–Hastings sampler, which gives a full posterior distribution and therefore easy uncertainty intervals on any derived quantity.

I expect the three methods to agree on the broad ranking — the best classes will look best and the worst will look worst under all three. Where they differ is in how they quantify preference and how they express uncertainty: counts gives a quick descriptive score, MLE gives point estimates with standard errors, and Bayes gives credible intervals on the things we actually care about, such as shares of preference.

Counts Analysis

The counts analysis is the simplest way to score the classes. For each class \(j\) we look only at the screens where it appeared, and compute the share of those appearances on which it was picked best and the share on which it was picked worst. The difference is a one-number score:

\[ \text{score}_j \;=\; \%\text{best}_j \;-\; \%\text{worst}_j . \]

A score near \(+1\) means a class is almost always chosen best when it is shown; near \(-1\) means it is almost always the worst; near \(0\) means it is either middle-of-the-pack or polarizing (picked best and worst about equally often). It uses no model — just tallies — which is exactly why it is a good first look.

Bootstrap confidence intervals. To attach uncertainty to these scores I resample respondents (not individual rows) with replacement 1,000 times, recompute every class’s score on each resample, and take the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles. Resampling whole respondents respects the fact that one person contributes many correlated screens.

From MaxDiff Data to MNL Choices

The counts analysis throws away information: it treats each screen’s best pick and worst pick in isolation and never uses the fact that an item was available but not chosen. The multinomial logit (MNL) model fixes this by modelling the choice process itself. (This is the conjoint/MNL framework from the week-5 deck.)

We give each class \(j\) a latent utility coefficient \(\beta_j\). On a screen showing the set \(\{a,b,c,d\}\), the probability that item \(b\) is the one picked best is the soft-max of the utilities over the shown set:

\[ P(\text{best} = b) \;=\; \frac{\exp(\beta_b)}{\exp(\beta_a)+\exp(\beta_b)+\exp(\beta_c)+\exp(\beta_d)} . \]

The respondent then picks a worst item from the three that remain after the best is removed. Picking the worst is just picking the best of the negated utilities, so

\[ P(\text{worst} = c \mid \text{best} = b) \;=\; \frac{\exp(-\beta_c)}{\exp(-\beta_a)+\exp(-\beta_c)+\exp(-\beta_d)} . \]

So each MaxDiff screen yields two MNL choice observations: one best-pick from the full set of four, and one worst-pick from the remaining three. Crucially, every shown-but-unchosen class now contributes to the likelihood through the denominators — that is the extra information the counts analysis ignored.

Identifiability. The soft-max is unchanged if we add the same constant to every \(\beta\), so only \(J-1 = 9\) of the ten coefficients are identified. I fix the first class as the reference, \(\beta_1 = 0\), and estimate \(\beta_2,\dots,\beta_{10}\) relative to it. Every reported \(\beta\) is therefore a utility relative to MGTA 403.

MNL via Maximum Likelihood

Summing the log-probabilities of the best and worst picks over all \(N \cdot T = 680\) tasks gives the log-likelihood

\[ \ell(\boldsymbol\beta) \;=\; \sum_{\text{tasks}} \Big[\, \beta_{j^*_b} - \log\!\!\sum_{j\in\text{shown}}\!\! e^{\beta_j} \;-\; \beta_{j^*_w} - \log\!\!\!\sum_{j\in\text{shown}\setminus\{j^*_b\}}\!\!\! e^{-\beta_j}\,\Big], \]

where \(j^*_b\) and \(j^*_w\) are the best and worst items chosen in that task. The first two terms are the best-pick log-probability; the last two are the worst-pick log-probability, with utilities flipped in sign and the chosen-best item removed from the set.

Show code 
# Log-likelihood. `beta9` holds beta_2..beta_10; beta_1 is pinned to 0 inside.
# Written as a plain loop over tasks so the math above maps line-for-line onto
# the code. Uses log(sum(exp(.))) directly; the utilities here are small enough
# that no extra log-sum-exp shifting is needed for stability.
loglik <- function(beta9) {
  beta <- c(0, beta9)                 # length-10 utility vector, beta_1 = 0
  total <- 0
  for (t in seq_len(n_tasks)) {
    shown <- TASK_ITEMS[[t]]
    b <- BEST[t]; w <- WORST[t]
    # best pick: soft-max over all four shown utilities
    total <- total + beta[b] - log(sum(exp(beta[shown])))
    # worst pick: soft-max over the three remaining, utilities negated
    remaining <- shown[shown != b]
    total <- total - beta[w] - log(sum(exp(-beta[remaining])))
  }
  total
}

To make the coefficients interpretable I convert them to shares of preference — what fraction of choices each class would win if all ten appeared on one screen:

\[ \hat s_j \;=\; \frac{\exp(\hat\beta_j)}{\sum_{k=1}^{10}\exp(\hat\beta_k)} . \]

MNL via Bayesian Estimation

Now I fit the same MNL model the Bayesian way (week-7 deck). Instead of a single point estimate, we characterise the whole posterior distribution of \(\boldsymbol\beta\),

\[ \pi(\boldsymbol\beta \mid \boldsymbol y) \;\propto\; L(\boldsymbol\beta)\cdot\pi(\boldsymbol\beta), \]

with a weakly informative prior \(\boldsymbol\beta \sim N(\boldsymbol 0,\,10\cdot I)\) (an SD of \(\sqrt{10}\approx 3.2\) on each coefficient — diffuse, but not absurd). Working on the log scale, the log-posterior is the log-likelihood plus the log of the normal prior density.

Show code 
log_post <- function(beta9) {
  loglik(beta9) + sum(dnorm(beta9, mean = 0, sd = sqrt(10), log = TRUE))
}

The sampler. A textbook random-walk Metropolis: propose a small Gaussian step from the current \(\boldsymbol\beta\), and accept it with probability \(\min\!\big(1,\, e^{\,\text{logpost}^\star - \text{logpost}}\big)\) (compared on the log scale against \(\log U\)). Reject and we stay put. Over many iterations the chain spends time in each region of \(\boldsymbol\beta\)-space in proportion to its posterior probability.

Show code 
metropolis <- function(beta0, n_iter, step) {
  K      <- length(beta0)
  draws  <- matrix(NA_real_, n_iter, K)
  beta   <- beta0
  lp     <- log_post(beta)
  accept <- 0
  for (t in seq_len(n_iter)) {
    beta_star <- beta + rnorm(K, 0, step)      # symmetric random-walk proposal
    lp_star   <- log_post(beta_star)
    if (log(runif(1)) < lp_star - lp) {        # accept/reject on the log scale
      beta <- beta_star; lp <- lp_star; accept <- accept + 1
    }
    draws[t, ] <- beta
  }
  list(draws = draws, accept_rate = accept / n_iter)
}

Tuning the step size. We want an acceptance rate of roughly 25–40%. Too big a step and almost every proposal is rejected (the chain gets stuck); too small and it crawls. The rule of thumb: if acceptance is too low, shrink the step; too high, grow it. A few short trial runs locate the sweet spot:

What the MNL adds over counts. The counts score only uses the best and worst picks. The MNL additionally uses every shown-but-not-picked item: each appearance in a denominator is evidence that a class lost to the others on that screen. The model therefore extracts more information from the same data and produces estimates with a coherent probabilistic interpretation (utilities and shares) rather than a bare descriptive score.

What Bayes adds over MLE. For the \(\beta\)’s, almost nothing here — the estimates coincide. The advantage is uncertainty on derived quantities. A credible interval on a share of preference is trivial under Bayes: compute the share for each MCMC draw and read off the quantiles. Getting the same interval from the MLE requires the delta method (propagating the Hessian through the nonlinear soft-max). The Bayesian machinery also extends naturally to richer questions — market-simulation scenarios, “what if we added a new class?”, or a hierarchical model for individual-level preferences.

Discussion

The substantive finding. MSBA students clearly prefer MGTA 495 Marketing Analytics above all other core classes — by a wide and statistically clear margin under every method. Behind it sits a tier of hands-on analytics electives (453 Business Analytics, 455 Customer Analytics, 452 Large Data), while the foundational and process-oriented courses (444 Analytics Consulting, 451 Marketing/Finance/Operations, 403 AI Math/Programming) are the least preferred. The most plausible reading is that students gravitate toward applied, tool-and-technique electives and rate required-foundation or breadth/process courses lower. The mild surprise is how low the 403 AI Math/Programming course lands given the field’s enthusiasm for AI — likely because it is a required summer prerequisite experienced as work to get through, not an elective chosen out of interest.

The methodological finding — which tool for which job:

  • Counts — a fast, model-free descriptive summary. Reach for it when you need a quick read or an executive dashboard, and you are happy with a relative score rather than a probability.
  • MLE MNL — rigorous point estimates with classical standard errors, and a proper choice model underneath. Reach for it when you need a single defensible number to report and trust large-sample theory for its uncertainty.
  • Bayesian MNL — when you want uncertainty on derived quantities (shares, market-share simulations, “what if we added a class?” scenarios) without delta-method gymnastics, or when you want to extend the model hierarchically to estimate each student’s individual preferences.

Caveats. The survey was taken by a self-selected sample of current MSBA students, so these preferences may not generalize to other programs, future cohorts, or prospective students. Responses can also be shaped by where a class falls in the program sequence, which instructor teaches it, prior coursework, and even item-presentation order within the survey. The MNL here is a single, aggregate (homogeneous) model — it assumes everyone shares the same utilities, which surely understates real heterogeneity in taste.

What I would do next. The natural extension is a hierarchical (mixed) MNL that gives each respondent their own \(\boldsymbol\beta\) drawn from a population distribution. That would reveal whether the apparent consensus hides distinct student segments (say, “marketing-leaning” vs “engineering-leaning” students) and would let us simulate how preference shares shift if a new class were added or an existing one dropped — the kind of forward-looking question the aggregate model cannot answer.

## Introduction

This report measures how strongly MSBA students at UCSD Rady prefer one core
class over another. The data come from a **MaxDiff** survey (also called
*Best–Worst Scaling*). On each screen the respondent sees a small subset of
classes — here, four — and is asked to pick the **single most-preferred** and
the **single least-preferred** class in that set. Repeating this over many
screens, and across many respondents, builds up a detailed picture of relative
preference.

Why MaxDiff rather than, say, asking people to rate each class 1–10? Humans are
reliable at *trade-offs* — "this one is better than that one" — but unreliable
at calibrating an absolute numeric scale. One respondent's "7" is another's
"9", some people never use the extremes, and ratings drift over the course of a
survey. By forcing a best and a worst pick out of a small set, MaxDiff removes
scale-use bias and makes everyone genuinely differentiate between the items.

The ten items here are the ten core MSBA classes. I estimate students' relative
preference for them three ways, and compare what each method tells us:

1. A simple **counts analysis** — the % of times a class is picked *best* minus
   the % of times it is picked *worst*.
2. The **multinomial logit (MNL)** model fit by **maximum likelihood** (MLE),
   which models the best/worst choices explicitly and gives classical standard
   errors.
3. The same MNL model fit by **Bayesian** methods, via a random-walk
   Metropolis–Hastings sampler, which gives a full posterior distribution and
   therefore easy uncertainty intervals on any derived quantity.

I expect the three methods to **agree on the broad ranking** — the best classes
will look best and the worst will look worst under all three. Where they differ
is in *how* they quantify preference and *how* they express uncertainty: counts
gives a quick descriptive score, MLE gives point estimates with standard errors,
and Bayes gives credible intervals on the things we actually care about, such as
shares of preference.

## Counts Analysis

The **counts analysis** is the simplest way to score the classes. For each class
$j$ we look only at the screens where it appeared, and compute the share of
those appearances on which it was picked *best* and the share on which it was
picked *worst*. The difference is a one-number score:

$$
\text{score}_j \;=\; \%\text{best}_j \;-\; \%\text{worst}_j .
$$

A score near $+1$ means a class is almost always chosen best when it is shown;
near $-1$ means it is almost always the worst; near $0$ means it is either
middle-of-the-pack or polarizing (picked best and worst about equally often).
It uses no model — just tallies — which is exactly why it is a good first look.

```{r counts-table}
counts <- md |>

            worst = sum(resp == -1),
            .groups = "drop") |>

         pct_worst = worst / shown,
```

## From MaxDiff Data to MNL Choices

The counts analysis throws away information: it treats each screen's best pick
and worst pick in isolation and never uses the fact that an item was *available
but not chosen*. The **multinomial logit (MNL)** model fixes this by modelling
the choice process itself. (This is the conjoint/MNL framework from the week-5
deck.)

We give each class $j$ a latent **utility coefficient** $\beta_j$. On a screen
showing the set $\{a,b,c,d\}$, the probability that item $b$ is the one picked
*best* is the soft-max of the utilities over the shown set:

$$
P(\text{best} = b) \;=\; \frac{\exp(\beta_b)}{\exp(\beta_a)+\exp(\beta_b)+\exp(\beta_c)+\exp(\beta_d)} .
$$

The respondent then picks a *worst* item from the **three that remain** after
the best is removed. Picking the worst is just picking the best of the
*negated* utilities, so

$$
P(\text{worst} = c \mid \text{best} = b) \;=\; \frac{\exp(-\beta_c)}{\exp(-\beta_a)+\exp(-\beta_c)+\exp(-\beta_d)} .
$$

So **each MaxDiff screen yields two MNL choice observations**: one best-pick from
the full set of four, and one worst-pick from the remaining three. Crucially,
every shown-but-unchosen class now contributes to the likelihood through the
denominators — that is the extra information the counts analysis ignored.

**Identifiability.** The soft-max is unchanged if we add the same constant to
every $\beta$, so only $J-1 = 9$ of the ten coefficients are identified. I fix
the first class as the reference, $\beta_1 = 0$, and estimate
$\beta_2,\dots,\beta_{10}$ relative to it. Every reported $\beta$ is therefore a
utility *relative to MGTA 403*.

```{r reshape-tasks}
# One row per task is the natural unit for the likelihood: store the four item
# numbers shown, plus which one was best and which was worst.
tasks <- md |>
  group_by(rid, task) |>
  summarise(items = list(item),
            best  = item[resp ==  1],
            worst = item[resp == -1],
            .groups = "drop")

TASK_ITEMS <- lapply(tasks$items, as.integer)   # list of length-4 integer vectors
BEST  <- as.integer(tasks$best)                 # best item per task
WORST <- as.integer(tasks$worst)                # worst item per task
n_tasks <- length(BEST)
```


## MNL via Maximum Likelihood

Summing the log-probabilities of the best and worst picks over all
$N \cdot T = `r n_tasks`$ tasks gives the log-likelihood

$$
\ell(\boldsymbol\beta) \;=\; \sum_{\text{tasks}}
\Big[\, \beta_{j^*_b} - \log\!\!\sum_{j\in\text{shown}}\!\! e^{\beta_j}
\;-\; \beta_{j^*_w} - \log\!\!\!\sum_{j\in\text{shown}\setminus\{j^*_b\}}\!\!\! e^{-\beta_j}\,\Big],
$$

where $j^*_b$ and $j^*_w$ are the best and worst items chosen in that task. The
first two terms are the best-pick log-probability; the last two are the
worst-pick log-probability, with utilities flipped in sign and the chosen-best
item removed from the set.

```{r loglik}
# Log-likelihood. `beta9` holds beta_2..beta_10; beta_1 is pinned to 0 inside.
# Written as a plain loop over tasks so the math above maps line-for-line onto
# the code. Uses log(sum(exp(.))) directly; the utilities here are small enough
# that no extra log-sum-exp shifting is needed for stability.
loglik <- function(beta9) {
  beta <- c(0, beta9)                 # length-10 utility vector, beta_1 = 0
  total <- 0
  for (t in seq_len(n_tasks)) {
    shown <- TASK_ITEMS[[t]]
    b <- BEST[t]; w <- WORST[t]
    # best pick: soft-max over all four shown utilities
    total <- total + beta[b] - log(sum(exp(beta[shown])))
    # worst pick: soft-max over the three remaining, utilities negated
    remaining <- shown[shown != b]
    total <- total - beta[w] - log(sum(exp(-beta[remaining])))
  }
  total
}
```

Maximizing with `optim` (BFGS, `fnscale = -1` to turn the minimizer into a
maximizer) and asking for the Hessian:

```{r mle-fit}
fit <- optim(par = rep(0, 9), fn = loglik,
             method = "BFGS", hessian = TRUE,
             control = list(fnscale = -1))

cat("Converged (0 = yes):", fit$convergence,
    " | log-likelihood at optimum:", round(fit$value, 2), "\n")

# Standard errors from the inverse of the negative Hessian (the observed
# information matrix). With fnscale = -1, optim returns the Hessian of the
# log-likelihood itself, so -Hessian is the (positive-definite) information.
se9 <- sqrt(diag(solve(-fit$hessian)))

beta_hat <- c(0, fit$par)
se_hat   <- c(0, se9)
```

To make the coefficients interpretable I convert them to **shares of
preference** — what fraction of choices each class would win if all ten appeared
on one screen:

$$
\hat s_j \;=\; \frac{\exp(\hat\beta_j)}{\sum_{k=1}^{10}\exp(\hat\beta_k)} .
$$

```{r mle-table}
mle <- tibble(item = 1:10, beta = beta_hat, se = se_hat) |>
  mutate(share = exp(beta) / sum(exp(beta))) |>
  left_join(item_labels, by = "item") |>
  arrange(desc(share)) |>
```

## MNL via Bayesian Estimation

Now I fit the *same* MNL model the Bayesian way (week-7 deck). Instead of a
single point estimate, we characterise the whole **posterior** distribution of
$\boldsymbol\beta$,

$$
\pi(\boldsymbol\beta \mid \boldsymbol y) \;\propto\; L(\boldsymbol\beta)\cdot\pi(\boldsymbol\beta),
$$

with a weakly informative prior $\boldsymbol\beta \sim N(\boldsymbol 0,\,10\cdot I)$
(an SD of $\sqrt{10}\approx 3.2$ on each coefficient — diffuse, but not absurd).
Working on the log scale, the log-posterior is the log-likelihood plus the log
of the normal prior density.

```{r log-post}
log_post <- function(beta9) {
  loglik(beta9) + sum(dnorm(beta9, mean = 0, sd = sqrt(10), log = TRUE))
}
```

**The sampler.** A textbook random-walk Metropolis: propose a small Gaussian
step from the current $\boldsymbol\beta$, and accept it with probability
$\min\!\big(1,\, e^{\,\text{logpost}^\star - \text{logpost}}\big)$ (compared on
the log scale against $\log U$). Reject and we stay put. Over many iterations the
chain spends time in each region of $\boldsymbol\beta$-space in proportion to its
posterior probability.

```{r mh-sampler}
metropolis <- function(beta0, n_iter, step) {
  K      <- length(beta0)
  draws  <- matrix(NA_real_, n_iter, K)
  beta   <- beta0
  lp     <- log_post(beta)
  accept <- 0
  for (t in seq_len(n_iter)) {
    beta_star <- beta + rnorm(K, 0, step)      # symmetric random-walk proposal
    lp_star   <- log_post(beta_star)
    if (log(runif(1)) < lp_star - lp) {        # accept/reject on the log scale
      beta <- beta_star; lp <- lp_star; accept <- accept + 1
    }
    draws[t, ] <- beta
  }
  list(draws = draws, accept_rate = accept / n_iter)
}
```

**Tuning the step size.** We want an acceptance rate of roughly 25–40%. Too big
a step and almost every proposal is rejected (the chain gets stuck); too small
and it crawls. The rule of thumb: if acceptance is too low, shrink the step; too
high, grow it. A few short trial runs locate the sweet spot:

```{r mh-tune}
tune <- map_dfr(c(0.20, 0.10, 0.07, 0.05), function(s) {
  r <- metropolis(fit$par, n_iter = 2000, step = s)
  tibble(step = s, accept_rate = round(r$accept_rate, 3))
})
kable(tune, col.names = c("Step size", "Acceptance rate"),
      caption = "Step-size tuning: a step of 0.07 lands in the 25–40% target") |>
  kable_styling(full_width = FALSE)
```

A **step of 0.07** sits squarely in the target band, so I run the real chain
with it: 20,000 iterations, discarding the first 25% (5,000) as burn-in.

**What the MNL adds over counts.** The counts score only uses the best and worst
*picks*. The MNL additionally uses every *shown-but-not-picked* item: each
appearance in a denominator is evidence that a class lost to the others on that
screen. The model therefore extracts more information from the same data and
produces estimates with a coherent probabilistic interpretation (utilities and
shares) rather than a bare descriptive score.

**What Bayes adds over MLE.** For the $\beta$'s, almost nothing here — the
estimates coincide. The advantage is **uncertainty on derived quantities**. A
credible interval on a share of preference is trivial under Bayes: compute the
share for each MCMC draw and read off the quantiles. Getting the same interval
from the MLE requires the delta method (propagating the Hessian through the
nonlinear soft-max). The Bayesian machinery also extends naturally to richer
questions — market-simulation scenarios, "what if we added a new class?", or a
hierarchical model for *individual-level* preferences.


## Discussion

**The substantive finding.** MSBA students clearly prefer *MGTA 495 Marketing
Analytics* above all other core classes — by a wide and statistically clear
margin under every method. Behind it sits a tier of hands-on analytics electives
(*453 Business Analytics*, *455 Customer Analytics*, *452 Large Data*), while the
foundational and process-oriented courses (*444 Analytics Consulting*, *451
Marketing/Finance/Operations*, *403 AI Math/Programming*) are the least
preferred. The most plausible reading is that students gravitate toward applied,
tool-and-technique electives and rate required-foundation or
breadth/process courses lower. The mild surprise is how low the *403 AI
Math/Programming* course lands given the field's enthusiasm for AI — likely
because it is a required summer prerequisite experienced as work to get through,
not an elective chosen out of interest.

**The methodological finding** — which tool for which job:

- **Counts** — a fast, model-free descriptive summary. Reach for it when you
  need a quick read or an executive dashboard, and you are happy with a relative
  score rather than a probability.
- **MLE MNL** — rigorous point estimates with classical standard errors, and a
  proper choice model underneath. Reach for it when you need a single defensible
  number to report and trust large-sample theory for its uncertainty.
- **Bayesian MNL** — when you want uncertainty on *derived* quantities (shares,
  market-share simulations, "what if we added a class?" scenarios) without
  delta-method gymnastics, or when you want to extend the model hierarchically to
  estimate each student's individual preferences.

**Caveats.** The survey was taken by a self-selected sample of current MSBA
students, so these preferences may not generalize to other programs, future
cohorts, or prospective students. Responses can also be shaped by where a class
falls in the program sequence, which instructor teaches it, prior coursework,
and even item-presentation order within the survey. The MNL here is a single,
*aggregate* (homogeneous) model — it assumes everyone shares the same utilities,
which surely understates real heterogeneity in taste.

**What I would do next.** The natural extension is a **hierarchical (mixed) MNL**
that gives each respondent their own $\boldsymbol\beta$ drawn from a population
distribution. That would reveal whether the apparent consensus hides distinct
student segments (say, "marketing-leaning" vs "engineering-leaning" students) and
would let us simulate how preference shares shift if a new class were added or an
existing one dropped — the kind of forward-looking question the aggregate model
cannot answer.
